Why Seed Grading?

In one of our first talks with canola farmers, namely craig, it was identified that we had to do something about canola grading. We heard stories about farmers bringing their seeds into 2 different grain elevators (where they grade the seeds then bring the seeds to the crushers), and got two different grades for his seeds from the same sample. This has an enormous impact on farmers because selling grade 1 seeds gives a farmer their investment back. But being a Canola grower and selling grade 2 seeds will give him around half the money. Grade 2 seeds and lower can only really be used to produce meal due to their chlorophyll content. A farmer that produces anything but grade 1 seeds will lose a lot of money.



Seeds are sampled randomly, but are graded by the human eye. The human eye is a marvel, but detecting exact colours of all seeds perfectly, is difficult. To help farmers, and all those affected by poor grading practices, we built a tool that standardizes the grading of the seeds. This way, farmers and grain elevators will get the exact same grade every single time. We will try to first implement our method in Farmer Store Things, to get farmers used to it and iterate our project for usability. Another advantage of starting in the farmer store things, is that we can continuously update our project by reaching a large user base and buying the time to have a finalized product that runs fast and lasts a long time.

What has been done?

To understand what has been done, we talked to the Canada Grains Commission and their chief scientists/graders. We learned that there has been attempts to create a tool to measure chlorophyll quickly. They created a tool that costed around 22000$ and took over 2 minutes to run. We learned that to have a successful product, we needed it to be faster. The cost was less of a concern than the speed of operation. The method was to grind the seeds then run it through a NIR spectrometer. This method homogenizes the seeds then measures the amount of green in this mixture, which is extremely precise. But due to the need of grinding the seeds, it becomes much slower. Our project will use simple cameras which makes the green readings less quantitative and more qualitative, further iterations may use a machine vision camera which can be customized to work excessively well in the environment we give it. Due to DGR classification being a qualitative problem, this makes our approach much stronger.
